Hedge Analysis Report

A Comprehensive Analysis of Hedging Patterns by Gender and Language

INTRODUCTION: UNDERSTANDING HEDGES

What are Hedges? Hedges are linguistic devices that express uncertainty, politeness, or tentativeness in communication. They allow speakers to soften statements, show modesty, or indicate degrees of certainty.

Examples of Hedges in Context

1. "About" - Approximation and Uncertainty
"I have about two years of experience with teaching individuals how to become an English speaker." - Tyesh B.
"I've been teaching Chinese for about two years." - Kai Y.

Function: Indicates approximate time periods, showing modesty about exact experience.

2. "Would" - Conditional Politeness
"I would love to meet you, and hear your story." - Rose R.
"I would be more than happy to tutor you on your speaking skills." - James J.

Function: Expresses polite willingness and creates a softer, more approachable tone.

3. "Around" - Approximation
"I have been teaching for around 8 years!" - Kathryn R.
"I have around 5 years of on-line ESL teaching experience." - Charles H.

Function: Provides approximate quantities, avoiding overly precise claims.

4. "Apparent" - Qualified Certainty
"Learning is magnified by high Repetition in a relaxed friendly cheerful optimistic environment... so it distracts the conscious mind from focus on 'trying to learn' so teaching goes direct into the Unconscious mind. Thats done by apparent 'fun learning' for all age groups." - John W.

Function: Suggests something seems to be the case without absolute certainty.

5. "Possibly" - Uncertainty
"I am also currently learning Chinese, so I can possibly help with Chinese students improving their English." - Daniel M.

Function: Expresses potential ability without making definitive promises.

6. "Mainly" - Qualification
"For each different level of students, we will have different materials for lessons, we will talk like friends and laughs a lot! Mainly is for you to feel comfortable to talk." - Liting H.

Function: Indicates primary purpose while allowing for other possibilities.

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS

Key Results:
  • Gender Analysis: No significant difference in hedge usage between male and female tutors (p = 0.7257)
  • Language Analysis: Significant difference in hedge usage between Chinese and English tutors (p = 0.0227), with English tutors using more hedges
  • Most Common Hedges: "around", "would", and "about" are the most frequently used hedge words across all groups
  • Individual Hedge Analysis: No individual hedge word shows significant association with gender or language when tested separately
  • Functional Patterns: Hedges serve different communicative functions - approximation ("about", "around"), politeness ("would"), and uncertainty ("possibly", "apparent")
