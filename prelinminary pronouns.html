Correlation Analysis: Pronoun Usage and Hourly Rate (Chinese Male Tutors)

Key Findings

1. Negative Correlation: First-Person Singular Pronouns

  • Singular first-person pronoun density shows a significant negative correlation with hourly rates (r = -0.244, p = 0.002**)
    • Tutors who use more "I/me/my" per 1000 words tend to charge lower prices
    • This effect is robust across both Pearson (r = -0.244**) and Spearman (ρ = -0.289***) tests
    • Interpretation: Excessive self-focus may signal less professional or client-centered communication
  • Overall pronoun density also negatively correlates with price (r = -0.197, p = 0.014*)
    • Higher overall pronoun usage is associated with lower rates
    • Spearman correlation is stronger (ρ = -0.228, p = 0.004**)

2. Positive Correlation: Plural First-Person & Second-Person Pronouns

  • Plural first-person pronouns (raw count) show a positive correlation (r = 0.174, p = 0.031*)
    • Using "we/us/our" is associated with higher prices
    • Interpretation: Inclusive language may signal collaboration and partnership with students
  • Second-person pronouns (raw count) also positively correlate (r = 0.175, p = 0.031*)
    • More use of "you/your" relates to higher rates
    • Interpretation: Student-centered, direct address may be valued by clients
    • Note: The density measure (per 1000 words) is not significant, suggesting absolute usage matters more than relative frequency

3. Text Length

  • Profile length shows a weak positive trend (r = 0.135, p = 0.096 ns)
    • Longer profiles tend toward higher prices, but not significantly
    • May reflect greater effort or professionalism

Interpretation

Higher-priced Chinese male tutors tend to:

  • Use fewer singular first-person pronouns ("I", "me", "my")
  • Employ more plural first-person pronouns ("we", "us", "our")
  • Address students directly with second-person pronouns ("you", "your")
  • Write with lower overall pronoun density (more concrete content)

Lower-priced tutors tend to:

  • Overuse "I/me/my" (self-focused language)
  • Have higher pronoun-to-content ratios (less substantive information)

Implications

This pattern suggests that professional positioning matters: tutors who frame their teaching as a collaborative partnership ("we will learn together") and focus on the student ("you will achieve...") rather than themselves ("I have experience...") command higher rates. The linguistic shift from egocentric to allocentric discourse may signal greater pedagogical maturity and client-centeredness.
